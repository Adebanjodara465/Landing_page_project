THE BANSHEE

From the Carpathian shadows, where life and death entwine.

October 22, 1888 Sister Evelyn Rowan

The Banshee, known in the oldest Gaelic as bean sídhe — the woman of the fae hills — is less a spirit of vengeance than of grief. Her origin lies shrouded in tragedy: the unburied maiden, the wronged wife, or the child-mother cast from grace. Condemned to mourn eternally, she roams places of stillness and sorrow, bound to families by ancient bloodlines. Her cry is not a mere scream but a dirge — a song too mournful for human ears. Those who hear it describe a chill that seeps into the soul, an ache that precedes death itself. Sometimes she appears at a window, weeping softly; other times, her voice alone fills the night while no form is seen.

Witnesses recount her garments fluttering like pale smoke, her hair white as frost, her eyes glowing with tears that never fall. Some scholars believe her wail cleanses the soul of the dying, allowing passage to the next realm. Others insist she feeds upon the grief she heralds, sustained by the sorrow she spreads. It is said that to show her kindness — to whisper a prayer or leave milk at the threshold — may earn her mercy. But those who mock her cries are doomed to die with their own voice stolen in terror.

Classification:Ethereal Harbinger
Threat Level: Moderate — psychological and spiritual hazard
